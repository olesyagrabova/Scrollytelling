Meet Martin.
He is the European average.
Martin's alarm goes off at 8am. Before he sits up, his thumb is already moving – swiping through Instagram Reels a friend shared last night.
He opens TikTok in the bathroom and continues scrolling.
A video shows explosions in the Middle East. He swipes. A cooking hack. Swipe. A dog video. Swipe. Ten minutes pass.
While getting dressed, YouTube plays in the background – a video about cryptocurrency, which he's not really listening to.
On the bus to university, he's back on TikTok, occasionally switching to check Instagram stories and respond to messages on WhatsApp.
During a 90-minute lecture on Political Theory he unlocks his phone twenty times.
By the time Martin returns home and lies in bed at night, still scrolling, he has spent approximately 6 hours staring at his smartphone screen.
Martin is not an exception.
He is the average European university student —
and he's addicted to social media.

Chapter 02

49% say they spend too much time on social media

Self-perception

Martin knows. So does everyone around him.

When students were asked how they perceived the time they spent on various activities, social media came top by a wide margin. Almost half – 49% – said they spend too much time on it. [1]

The gender split is notable: 33% of women say this, versus 16% of men.

How students perceive their time use (%)
Social media too much 49%, exercise too little 34%, sleep too little 28%.

They also know what they're missing. Students say they exercise too little (34%) and sleep too little (28%). They know. But knowing is not enough.

This intention-behaviour gap – wanting to use less, being unable to stop – is one of the defining features of addictive behaviour. [2]

Chapter 04

Measuring addiction: the Smartphone Addiction Scale

Clinical measurement

A validated instrument reveals widespread problematic use

The survey administered the validated Smartphone Addiction Scale (SAS-SV), a 10-item clinical instrument developed by Kwon et al. (2013). [4] It measures six dimensions: daily-life disturbance, withdrawal, loss of control, overuse, tolerance, and cyberspace-oriented relationships.

Mean SAS-SV scores cluster between 26 and 30 on a scale of 60. Clinical thresholds are set at 31 for males and 33 for females. A substantial share of this population is at or approaching those thresholds.

One finding challenges conventional assumptions: older male students (25–30) consistently score higher than younger female peers. [1] Early research suggested young women were the most at-risk group. This reversal suggests that heavy use may normalise and intensify as students move deeper into digital-native adult life — and that current interventions targeting young women may be systematically missing a population that is quietly accumulating the highest risk scores.

The evidence

Students using social media for more than three hours daily are 10 percentage points more likely to experience frequent depression or anxiety than those using it for less than one hour. [5] Three hours. The average in our survey is six.

Poor mental health imposes an annual economic burden of approximately €76 billion on European economies — equivalent to around 6% of total health budgets, or roughly the entire annual healthcare spending of Belgium.

— OECD (2026) [6]

Longitudinal research by Poulain et al. [7] reinforces the direction of the effect: usage exceeding three hours per day is significantly associated with lower quality-of-life scores, with a measurably stronger effect in girls than boys.

Chapter 06

What measures have been introduced so far

Policy landscape

Intervention is possible, enforceable, and does not require perfect evidence

Before discussing what could be done, it is worth establishing what has been tried.

🇫🇷
Ban · 2018 / 2024

France: Smartphone ban in schools

France banned smartphones in primary and middle schools in 2018 and extended the measure in 2024. [8] A government evaluation found that students in affected schools reported improved concentration and reduced anxiety. The measure was politically feasible and administratively simple — it required no platform cooperation.

🇦🇺
Legislation · Nov 2024

Australia: Under-16 social media ban

Australia passed legislation in November 2024 prohibiting children under 16 from holding social media accounts, with legal liability placed on platforms rather than users or parents. [9] The provision that shifted enforcement responsibility to platforms rather than families is considered the measure's central innovation.

None of these measures is a complete solution. Each addresses one dimension of a multi-dimensional problem. Together, they constitute a proof of concept: intervention is possible, enforceable, and does not require waiting for perfect evidence.

Chapter 07

What students want

Student demand

More than half support government intervention

Across all five countries, more than half of students support government intervention to reduce the harms of social media. [1] In every country without exception, combined support for national and EU-level regulation outstrips support for platform self-regulation and individual responsibility.

Student support for policy measures (%)
Increased transparency 63% support, awareness campaigns 65%, ban addictive features 51%, stricter age verification 64%, raised minimum age 59%.

French, Italian, and German students are consistently the most supportive of intervention across all five measures. This matters politically: these three countries represent the largest economies in the EU and the most significant voting blocs in the Council.

Students are not asking to be cut off from social media. They are asking for an honest environment where the rules of engagement are visible, where younger users are actively protected, and where platforms bear legal responsibility for measurable outcomes. That is not a radical demand.
